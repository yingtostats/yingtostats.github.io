### Estimate $\psi$ via Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE)

By Theorem 4.3 of Jiang (2007), suppose that $V$ is known and
$$
E(\dot{\mu}^{\prime}V^{-1}\dot{\mu})
$$
is non-singular. Then the optimal estimating function in class $H$ is
$$
G^{\ast} = \dot{\mu}^{\prime}V^{-1}(y-\mu),
$$
that is, with

$$
A = A^{\ast} = \dot{\mu}^{\prime}V^{-1}.
$$

The optimality is defined similarly to the univariate case. For nonnegative definite matrices, define $A \geq B$ if $A-B$ is nonnegative definite. Under this order, $G^{\ast}$ maximizes the generalized information criterion:

$$I(G) = \{E(\dot{G})\}^{\prime} \{E(GG^{\prime})\}^{-1} \{E(\dot{G})\}$$

where
$$
\dot{G} = \frac{\partial G}{\partial \theta^{\prime}}.
$$

For the longitudinal GLMM, the corresponding estimating equation can be written as

$$
G^{\ast} = \sum_{i=1}^{m} \dot{\mu}_{i}^{\prime}V_{i}^{-1}(y_{i}-\mu_{i}) = 0.
$$

where

* $y_{i} = (y_{ij})_{1 \leq j \leq 4}$,
* $\mu_{i} = E(y_{i}) = (\mu_{ij})_{1 \leq j \leq 4}$,
* $V_i = \operatorname{Var}(y_i)$,
* and

<div>
$$
\dot{\mu}_{i} = \{\frac{\partial \mu_{ij}}{\partial \psi_{k}}\}_{1 \leq j \leq 4, 1 \leq k \leq 11} =
\begin{pmatrix}
        \frac{\partial \mu_{i1}}{\partial \beta_{0}} & \frac{\partial \mu_{i1}}{\partial \beta_{1}} & ... & \frac{\partial \mu_{i1}}{\partial \beta_{6}} & \frac{\partial \mu_{i1}}{\partial \sigma_{1}} & ... & \frac{\partial \mu_{i1}}{\partial \tau} \\
        . & . & & . & . & & . \\
       . & . & ... & . & . & ... & . \\  
        . & . & & . & . & & . \\
  \frac{\partial \mu_{i4}}{\partial \beta_{0}} & \frac{\partial \mu_{i4}}{\partial \beta_{1}} & ... & \frac{\partial \mu_{i4}}{\partial \beta_{6}} & \frac{\partial \mu_{i4}}{\partial \sigma_{1}} & ... & \frac{\partial \mu_{i4}}{\partial \tau}
\end{pmatrix}
$$</div>

In practice, $V_i$ ($1 \leq i \leq 59$) is unknown. Liang and Zeger (1986) proposed replacing $V_i$ with a working covariance matrix. Under regularity conditions, the resulting GEE estimator remains consistent even if the working covariance is misspecified.

For simplicity, take $V_i = I_4$ and solve

$$
G_{I}^{\ast}=\sum_{i=1}^{59} \dot{\mu}_{i}^{\prime}(y_{i}-\mu_{i}) = 0.
$$

We can derive $\mu_{i}$ and $\dot{\mu}_{i}$ analytically:

$$
E(y_{ij} \mid \alpha_i) = \exp(x_{ij}^{\prime}\beta + \alpha_{i1} + \alpha_{i2}v_j + \epsilon_{ij})
= \exp(x_{ij}^{\prime}\beta + \xi_{ij})
$$

where

$$
\xi_{ij} \sim N\left(0,\sigma_{1}^{2}+v_j^2\sigma_{2}^{2}+2v_j\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2+\tau^{2}\right).
$$

Using the law of total expectation and the moment-generating function of a normal random variable,

$$
\mu_{ij} = E\{\exp(x_{ij}^{\prime}\beta + \xi_{ij})\}
= \exp(x_{ij}^{\prime}\beta)E(e^{\xi_{ij}})
= \exp\left\{x_{ij}^{\prime}\beta + \frac{1}{2}\left(\sigma_{1}^{2}+v_j^2\sigma_{2}^{2}+2v_j\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2+\tau^2\right)\right\}.
$$

Let $x_{ijk}$ be the $k$th component of $x_{ij}$ ($1 \leq k \leq 7$). Then

<div>$$
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial \mu_{ij}}{\partial \beta_{k-1}} &= \mu_{ij}x_{ijk}, \quad 1 \leq k \leq 7 \\
\frac{\partial \mu_{ij}}{\partial \sigma_{1}} &= \mu_{ij}(\sigma_{1} + v_{j}\rho \sigma_{2}) \\
\frac{\partial \mu_{ij}}{\partial \sigma_{2}} &= \mu_{ij}(v_{j}^{2}\sigma_{2} + v_{j}\rho \sigma_{1}) \\
\frac{\partial \mu_{ij}}{\partial \rho} &= \mu_{ij}v_{j}\sigma_{1}\sigma_{2} \\
\frac{\partial \mu_{ij}}{\partial \tau } &= \mu_{ij}\tau
\end{align}
$$</div>

Now, solve for $G^{\ast}_{I} = 0$ with the constraints $\sigma_{1} > 0$, $\sigma_{2} > 0$,
$\rho \in [-1,1]$, $\beta \in \textbf{R}^{7}$. This is an 11-dimensional nonlinear equation.

### Numerical Solution in Practice

In practice, this system is solved by nonlinear optimization/root-finding with constraints. A common workflow is:

* Re-parameterize constrained variables if needed (for example, optimize over $\log\sigma_1$, $\log\sigma_2$, and $\operatorname{atanh}(\rho)$).
* Use sensible initialization from simpler models.
* Solve $G_I^{\ast}(\psi)=0$ with Newton-type updates or quasi-Newton optimization of $\|G_I^{\ast}(\psi)\|^2$.
* Use robust (sandwich) standard errors for inference.

### Why GEE Is Attractive for This Model

* Compared with full maximum likelihood, GEE is often computationally lighter for longitudinal data.
* GEE is robust to covariance misspecification: even with a misspecified working covariance, regression parameter estimates can remain consistent.
* In longitudinal studies, additional serial dependence may exist beyond what a GLMM captures through random effects. GEE can still provide reliable marginal inference in such settings.

### Takeaway

For this model, GEE provides a practical compromise: tractable computation, robustness to covariance misspecification, and interpretable marginal effects. The trade-off is that efficiency can depend on the quality of the working covariance choice.
